# Figure Captions

## Figure 1: Media Sentiment Toward US-China Trade Relations (2017-2025)

Temporal evolution of media sentiment across the study period, measured using VADER compound scores. Individual articles are shown as points (light blue), with the 60-day rolling mean displayed as a dark line. Vertical dashed lines indicate major policy transitions: Section 301 announcement (March 2018), Phase One signing (January 2020), Biden inauguration (January 2021), and semiconductor export controls (October 2022). Background shading distinguishes the five policy periods. The rolling mean reveals sentiment decline during the trade war escalation (P2), reaching its nadir during the Phase One period (P3), with recovery in subsequent periods. N = 569 articles.

## Figure 2: Mean Sentiment by Source and Policy Period

Heatmap displaying mean VADER compound scores across seven news sources and five policy periods. Cell color intensity indicates sentiment valence (blue = positive, red = negative), with cell annotations showing mean scores and sample sizes. The New York Times and Washington Post demonstrate consistently moderate-to-positive sentiment across periods, while the Wall Street Journal shows notable negativity during the Biden Review period (P4: M = -0.52, n = 4). Broadcast sources (PBS NewsHour, MSNBC, Fox News) have limited sample sizes and should be interpreted with caution.

## Figure 3: Comparison of Sentiment Analysis Methods

Scatter plot comparing VADER compound scores (x-axis) with Pysentimiento negative probability (y-axis) for all 569 articles, colored by policy period. The weak negative correlation (r = -0.203, p < .001) indicates substantial divergence between lexicon-based (VADER) and transformer-based (Pysentimiento) sentiment classification. Most articles cluster in the lower portion of the plot, reflecting Pysentimiento's tendency to classify news text as neutral (93.3% of corpus). This methodological divergence underscores the importance of method selection in sentiment analysis of political news content.

## Figure 4: Sentiment Trajectories by News Source

Small multiple visualization showing sentiment trajectories for the four major print sources: The New York Times (n = 182), The Washington Post (n = 148), Financial Times (n = 136), and The Wall Street Journal (n = 89). Points are colored by policy period, with black lines indicating 90-day rolling means where sufficient data exist. All sources show similar temporal patterns—elevated sentiment pre-trade war, decline during escalation, and partial recovery in later periods—though with source-specific variation in magnitude.

## Figure 5: Sentiment Distribution Across Policy Periods

Box plot comparing VADER compound score distributions across the five policy periods. The Phase One period (P3) shows the lowest median sentiment, while the Pre-Trade War (P1) and Tech Decoupling (P5) periods show the highest. One-way ANOVA confirms significant differences across periods (F = 4.830, p < .001). Box widths reflect interquartile ranges; whiskers extend to 1.5× IQR; outliers are shown as individual points.
